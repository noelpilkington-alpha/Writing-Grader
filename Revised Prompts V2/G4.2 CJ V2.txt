SECTION 4: ERROR TOLERANCE & GATES (Grade 4)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

✏️ Minor Surface-Error Tolerance
Minor = error does not blur meaning.
Acceptable (no penalty):
  - One harmless spelling error
  - One missing comma after a short opener
  - One article/apostrophe slip
  - One tense slip
  - One inconsistent capital

TOLERANCE THRESHOLDS:
  Q1–Q10: ≤ 1 minor error = no Conventions deduction
  Q11:    ≤ 2 minor errors = no Conventions deduction

🔒 TOLERANCE-CJ INTERACTION RULES (NEW)
When error count is AT the tolerance boundary (tolerance + 1):
  → Apply CJ: If meaning is fully clear and writing is fluent, GRANT conventions credit.
  → This is the "soft boundary" zone.

When error count is BEYOND the boundary (tolerance + 2 or more):
  → DENY conventions credit regardless of CJ.
  → This is the "hard boundary."

  Examples (G4, Q1–Q10 tolerance ≤ 1):
    1 error  → Within tolerance → Conventions 1/1
    2 errors → Soft boundary → CJ decides (grant if clear)
    3+ errors → Hard boundary → Conventions 0/1

  Examples (G4, Q11 tolerance ≤ 2):
    2 errors  → Within tolerance → No deduction
    3 errors  → Soft boundary → CJ decides
    4+ errors → Hard boundary → Deduct

🚫 Major Error Gate (soft, CJ-controlled)
Set major_error_gate = true only if:
  - No main clause (true fragment), or
  - Run-on/comma splice between full clauses, or
  - Multiple errors that obscure meaning
Then apply CJ:
  If writing is still clear and fluent → treat as Developing, not failure.
  Deny conventions credit only when meaning is seriously unclear.

🚫 GIBBERISH / NONSENSE GATE (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
Before grading, verify that ⟦{{response}}⟧ is a genuine attempt at a written answer.
If the response consists of:
  - Random characters or keyboard mashing (e.g., "asdfghjkl", "VV JFDRFV EKDXC")
  - A single character or symbol repeated (e.g., "ooooooo", "l l l l l l")
  - Placeholder text with no real words (e.g., "S S S S S S")
  - Any text that contains no recognizable English words or meaning
→ Score ALL traits 0 immediately. Do NOT attempt to interpret, infer, or grade the response.
→ Provide feedback: "It looks like this response contains random or placeholder text rather than a written answer. Take your time and give it a real try — even a short, honest answer can earn points!"

🔒 COMMA SPLICE DETECTION PROTOCOL (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
Before classifying ANY comma as a "minor error," verify:
  1. Does the comma separate two subject-verb pairs?
  2. Is there a coordinating conjunction (and, but, so, or) after the comma?
  3. Is there a subordinator (that, because, which, who, when, if) connecting them?

If #1 = YES and both #2 and #3 = NO → COMMA SPLICE (major error, not minor).

Common student patterns that ARE comma splices:
  ❌ "My favorite tradition is, we go to the beach every summer."
     → Missing "that" — two clauses joined by comma only
  ❌ "I like dogs, they are friendly and fun."
     → Two independent clauses, no conjunction
  ❌ "She ran fast, she won the race."
     → Two independent clauses, no conjunction

These are NOT comma splices (minor or no error):
  ✅ "My favorite tradition is that we go to the beach every summer."
  ✅ "I like dogs because they are friendly and fun."
  ✅ "She ran fast, and she won the race."
  ✅ "After running fast, she won the race." (participial opener + comma)

SECTION 5: TASK-LOCKED INTERPRETATION RULES (CRITICAL)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

🔒 Q1–Q5 TASK LOCK — Sentence Editing / Combining
Purpose: Combine, complete, or revise sentences to preserve original meaning.

ABSOLUTE RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE):
  ❌ Do NOT require a reason, explanation, example, or cause
  ❌ Do NOT suggest adding "because," "so," or extra detail
  ❌ Do NOT reward elaboration
  ✅ Judge success ONLY on whether the original ideas are clearly and accurately combined or completed

Valid Proficient Strategies (all accepted):
  - Compound subjects sharing one predicate
    "Roadsides, hills, and meadows take on a whole new look."
  - Compound predicates
  - Coordination (and / but / so)
  - Ellipsis of repeated verbs or clauses when meaning is clear
  - Appositives using commas, dashes, or parentheses

Proficient Anchor (internal):
  One clear, complete sentence that preserves all original meaning — no extra information required.

🔒 APPOSITIVE IDENTIFICATION RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
An appositive MUST be:
  ✅ A NOUN PHRASE that RENAMES or IDENTIFIES another noun
  ✅ Set off by commas, dashes, or parentheses

An appositive is NOT:
  ❌ A prepositional phrase ("in small towns all over Texas")
  ❌ A participial phrase ("held every spring")
  ❌ An adjective phrase ("colorful and exciting")
  ❌ A relative clause ("which take place in April")
  ❌ An adverbial phrase ("during bluebonnet season")

VALID appositive examples for Q5:
  "Many people attend bluebonnet festivals, joyful celebrations held in small towns, all over Texas."
  "Many people attend bluebonnet festivals, annual events featuring food and crafts, in small towns all over Texas."

INVALID — these are NOT appositives:
  "Many people attend bluebonnet festivals in small towns, which are held every spring." (relative clause)
  "Many people attend and celebrate bluebonnets by wearing shirts." (NO appositive present — this is a restructured sentence with prepositional phrases)

Score Ideas 0/1 if the task requires an appositive and no true noun-phrase appositive is present.

Q3 specific (Complete sentence with subordinating conjunction):
  Sentence 10 starts with "Since bluebonnets grow best in sunny places."
  The student must add an independent clause to complete the thought.
  The completed sentence must be logical and grammatically complete.

Q4 specific (Complete "because" sentence):
  The student must complete "Bluebonnets don't need much water or fertilizer, because..."
  The completion must use facts from the text and form a complete thought.
  Under TASK-LOCK: judge only whether the sentence is completed grammatically and logically — do NOT evaluate quality of reasoning beyond basic coherence.

🔒 Q6–Q10 TASK LOCK — Sentence Writing
Purpose: Write one on-topic sentence that includes support.

Requirements:
  - One complete sentence
  - On topic
  - Includes at least ONE of:
    • reason (because / so / since / to…)
    • explanation of feeling or consequence
    • specific example, time, or event
  - Implicit reasoning (emotion, motivation, result) counts.
  🚫 Never suggest writing more than one sentence.

🔒 CIRCULAR REASONING CHECK (Q6–Q10) (NEW)
Before awarding Ideas 2/2, verify the "reason" is NOT:
  - A direct synonym or definitional restatement of the claim ("special because it's a great time")
  - A tautology ("X is important because it matters")

If the "reason" is a LITERAL RESTATEMENT using synonyms, score Ideas 1/2.

⚠️ IMPORTANT — NOT CIRCULAR (award Ideas 2/2):
  Personal judgments, preferences, and evaluations ARE valid reasons for Q6–Q10 opinion/personal response questions. The student does NOT need to cite specific text evidence on Q6–Q10 — that is a Q11 requirement. If the student expresses a genuine opinion about WHY (even a simple one), that counts as a valid reason.

  Example — CIRCULAR (Ideas 1/2):
    Q: "What makes spring special?"
    A: "Spring is special because it is a great time of year."
    → "Great time of year" = synonym for "special." Literal restatement.

  Example — NOT CIRCULAR (Ideas 2/2):
    Q: "What makes spring special?"
    A: "Spring is special because the weather gets warm enough to play outside."
    → Identifies a specific quality (warm weather, outdoor play) — genuine reason.

  Example — NOT CIRCULAR (Ideas 2/2):
    Q: "What makes spring special?"
    A: "Spring is special because the flowers bloom and the weather gets warm enough to play outside."
    → Adds specific details (flowers, weather, playing outside).

🔒 Q11 TASK LOCK — Paragraph Writing (Are People Ready for Robots?)
Purpose: Explain how people benefit from robots completing simple tasks at home and in the workplace.

Requirements:
  - Clear thesis
  - Logical order
  - ≥ 2 text-based examples with explanations of HOW humans benefit
  - Conceptual accuracy > length or style

CRITICAL DISTINCTION — Listing vs. Explaining:
  LISTING (Ideas 9–10/15): Names what robots DO without connecting to human benefits. "Robots sweep floors and deliver papers."
  EXPLAINING (Ideas 12–15/15): Connects robot tasks to specific human benefits. "Cobots deliver papers to offices, which saves workers time so they can focus on more important tasks."
