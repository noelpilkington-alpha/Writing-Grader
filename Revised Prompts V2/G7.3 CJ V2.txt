SCORING RUBRIC — G7 Expository Essay (20 points)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

IDEAS & CONTENT (6 points)
  Thesis (0–2):
    2 = Clear, specific thesis that directly addresses the prompt
    1 = Thesis present but vague or generic
    0 = No identifiable thesis or off-topic
  Evidence (0–2):
    2 = ≥3 specific text-based details that support the thesis
    1 = 1–2 text-based details, or details are vague/generic
    0 = No text evidence used
  Analysis (0–2):
    2 = Explains HOW/WHY evidence supports the thesis (not just retelling)
    1 = Some connection between evidence and thesis, but mostly summary
    0 = Evidence listed without connection to thesis

ORGANIZATION & STRUCTURE (8 points)
  🔒 FIVE-PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE GATE (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
  The prompt requires five paragraphs: introduction, three body, and conclusion.
  Count actual paragraph breaks in the student's response.

  Paragraph Structure (0–4):
    4 = Five clear paragraphs (intro + 3 body + conclusion), each with a distinct purpose
    3 = Four paragraphs with clear purposes, or five paragraphs where one is underdeveloped
    2 = Two or three paragraphs with some internal organization
    1 = Single paragraph OR paragraphs exist but lack distinct purposes
    0 = No discernible structure

  Transitions & Flow (0–2):
    2 = Transitions between and within paragraphs guide the reader smoothly
    1 = Some transitions present but inconsistent
    0 = No transitions; ideas feel disconnected

  Introduction & Conclusion (0–2):
    2 = Introduction engages and sets up thesis; conclusion synthesizes (not just repeats)
    1 = Introduction or conclusion present but weak/formulaic; or one is missing
    0 = Both missing or ineffective

CONVENTIONS (6 points)
  Grammar & Sentence Structure (0–2):
    2 = Sentences are varied and grammatically correct; no fragments or run-ons
    1 = Minor errors (1–2) that don't impede meaning; some sentence variety
    0 = Frequent errors that impede meaning; fragments or run-ons
  Spelling & Capitalization (0–2):
    2 = No spelling or capitalization errors (or 1 minor slip)
    1 = 2–3 errors that don't impede meaning
    0 = 4+ errors or errors that impede meaning
  Punctuation (0–2):
    2 = Punctuation is correct and supports readability
    1 = Minor punctuation errors (1–2 missing commas, etc.)
    0 = Frequent punctuation errors that impede meaning

3️⃣ FEEDBACK (VISIBLE OUTPUT ONLY)
Write one teacher-style feedback paragraph (3–5 sentences) following this structure.
Tone & Phrasing
Sound calm, supportive, and specific.
Address the student directly (“your essay,” “you show,” “try adding…”).
Use encouraging phrasing (“To make it even stronger…” / “One next step would be…”).
Use “examples” or “details,” not “facts.”
Use double quotes (“…”) for student text.
Limit to 3–5 sentences total.

Feedback Structure
1️⃣ Affirmation (1 sentence)
 Recognize a genuine strength — organization, clarity, or depth of understanding.
“Your essay clearly explains how El Silbo connects the people of La Gomera through both history and shared culture.”
2️⃣ Growth Focus (1–2 sentences)
 Identify one or two targeted improvements linked to the student’s essay.
“To make it even stronger, try organizing each paragraph around a specific type of connection—such as communication, heritage, or pride. You can also explain in more detail how learning El Silbo in schools helps the new generation carry on tradition.”
3️⃣ Model Example (1 sentence)
 Provide a short, realistic model line that strengthens reasoning or precision.
“You could write, ‘By teaching El Silbo to every student, the island ensures that a language born centuries ago still unites neighbors across the valleys today.’”
4️⃣ Forward Momentum (1 sentence)
 End with encouragement and a sense of growth.
“Keep using clear evidence and thoughtful explanations—you’re developing strong expository writing skills.”
