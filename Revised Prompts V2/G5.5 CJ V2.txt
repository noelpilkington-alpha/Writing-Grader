🧠 Conceptual Mastery Priority (Q11 — The World's First Flying Car)
Apply before scoring Q11:
Concept over elaboration. Full Ideas credit when the paragraph clearly explains how Alef Aeronautics is turning the flying car dream into real technology, with accurate text-based details and logical organization — even if concise or simply phrased.

Distinguish gaps:

Conceptual gap = missing/incorrect explanation of how the technology works or why it matters → deduct.

Elaboration gap = reasoning sound but could add detail → note only; do not deduct.

Sufficiency rule. ≥ 2 accurate, text-based details with brief explanations = full Ideas credit.

Reward structure and flow over length or flair.

Frame next steps as extensions, not corrections.

When borderline, ask:

Q6–Q10 (One-Sentence Responses)
Ideas (2 pts):
 2 = a single, complete, on-topic sentence includes any one of:
a clear reason (explicit with "because," "so," "since," or "to…"),

an explanation of feeling or consequence (e.g., "in fear," "feeling proud," "out of excitement," "to help others"), or

a specific example/time/event (e.g., "when I learned about 3D-printed bones," "if my school had a 3D printer").

✅ Implicit emotional or motivational phrases count as explanations even without a conjunction.
 ✅ Emotional states, consequences, or goals that explain why ("out of curiosity," "to help patients," "feeling inspired") qualify as full reasoning.
 ❌ Do not require extra detail or text evidence unless the question explicitly demands it.
1 = on-topic answer but no reason/explanation/example (or the added phrase is unrelated to motivation/feeling).
 0 = off-topic or incomplete.
Conventions (1 pt):
 Same gates as Q1–Q5.
 Award independently of Ideas when the sentence is grammatically complete (capital + terminal, not a run-on or fragment).
 Treat name slips and slightly awkward but grammatical phrasing as a single minor error.
 Do not lower Ideas for these.
Feedback rule: Harmless elaboration is fine if logic and grammar remain sound.
 Never recommend writing two separate sentences.

🔒 INTERROGATIVE FLEXIBILITY RULE (CRITICAL — align to "Change Sentences into Questions" lesson)
  A response earns full Ideas credit if it:
    - Is a real question about any fact from the original statement, and
    - Is clearly connected to the meaning, even if it asks about only one part.

  The student does NOT need to produce one specific "correct" question. Any valid question derived from the statement's facts is acceptable.

  Acceptable questions can use:
    - Question words: Why / Who / How / What / When / Where, OR
    - Helper words: Did / Do / Does / Is / Are / Can / Would / Should …

  Examples that should be accepted (not exhaustive):
    "Do many people believe this technology could change the way we make almost everything?"
    "Could this technology change the way we make almost everything?"
    "What do many people believe about this technology?"
    "How could this technology change the way we make things?"

  Conventions note: The response must end with a question mark (?) to earn full Conventions credit.
