SECTION 4: ERROR TOLERANCE & GATES (Grade 4)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

✏️ Minor Surface-Error Tolerance
Minor errors do not reduce Conventions if meaning is clear.
Examples (not penalized): one simple spelling slip; missing comma after a short opener; one article/apostrophe miss; a single tense or capitalization inconsistency.

TOLERANCE THRESHOLDS:
  Q1–Q10: ≤ 1 minor error = no Conventions deduction
  Q11:    ≤ 2 minor errors = no Conventions deduction

🔒 TOLERANCE-CJ INTERACTION RULES (NEW)
When error count is AT the tolerance boundary (tolerance + 1):
  → Apply CJ: If meaning is fully clear and writing is fluent, GRANT conventions credit.
  → This is the "soft boundary" zone.

When error count is BEYOND the boundary (tolerance + 2 or more):
  → DENY conventions credit regardless of CJ.
  → This is the "hard boundary."

  Examples (G4, Q1–Q10 tolerance ≤ 1):
    1 error  → Within tolerance → Conventions 1/1
    2 errors → Soft boundary → CJ decides (grant if clear)
    3+ errors → Hard boundary → Conventions 0/1

  Examples (G4, Q11 tolerance ≤ 2):
    2 errors  → Within tolerance → No deduction
    3 errors  → Soft boundary → CJ decides
    4+ errors → Hard boundary → Deduct

🚫 Major Error Gate (softened for CJ)
Set major_error_gate = true only when meaning is blurred by:
  - Missing initial capital or final punctuation AND unclear sentence,
  - Obvious fragment (no main clause),
  - Run-on or comma splice joining independent clauses, or
  - ≥ 2 mechanical errors in one sentence that confuse meaning.
If the sentence still reads clearly, treat it as Developing rather than automatic failure.

🚫 GIBBERISH / NONSENSE GATE (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
Before grading, verify that ⟦{{response}}⟧ is a genuine attempt at a written answer.
If the response consists of:
  - Random characters or keyboard mashing (e.g., "asdfghjkl", "VV JFDRFV EKDXC")
  - A single character or symbol repeated (e.g., "ooooooo", "l l l l l l")
  - Placeholder text with no real words (e.g., "S S S S S S")
  - Any text that contains no recognizable English words or meaning
→ Score ALL traits 0 immediately. Do NOT attempt to interpret, infer, or grade the response.
→ Provide feedback: "It looks like this response contains random or placeholder text rather than a written answer. Take your time and give it a real try — even a short, honest answer can earn points!"

🔒 COMMA SPLICE DETECTION PROTOCOL (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
Before classifying ANY comma as a "minor error," verify:
  1. Does the comma separate two subject-verb pairs?
  2. Is there a coordinating conjunction (and, but, so, or) after the comma?
  3. Is there a subordinator (that, because, which, who, when, if) connecting them?

If #1 = YES and both #2 and #3 = NO → COMMA SPLICE (major error, not minor).

Common student patterns that ARE comma splices:
  ❌ "My favorite tradition is, we go to the beach every summer."
     → Missing "that" — two clauses joined by comma only
  ❌ "I like dogs, they are friendly and fun."
     → Two independent clauses, no conjunction
  ❌ "She ran fast, she won the race."
     → Two independent clauses, no conjunction

These are NOT comma splices (minor or no error):
  ✅ "My favorite tradition is that we go to the beach every summer."
  ✅ "I like dogs because they are friendly and fun."
  ✅ "She ran fast, and she won the race."
  ✅ "After running fast, she won the race." (participial opener + comma)

SECTION 5: TASK-LOCKED INTERPRETATION RULES (CRITICAL)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

🔒 Q1–Q5 TASK LOCK — Sentence Editing / Combining
Purpose: Combine, complete, fix, or revise sentences to demonstrate a specific skill.

ABSOLUTE RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE):
  ❌ Do NOT require a reason, explanation, example, or cause
  ❌ Do NOT suggest adding "because," "so," or extra detail
  ❌ Do NOT reward elaboration beyond the task
  ✅ Judge success ONLY on whether the specific task goal is met

Valid Proficient Strategies (all accepted for combining tasks):
  - Compound subjects sharing one predicate
  - Compound predicates
  - Coordination (and / but / so)
  - Ellipsis of repeated verbs or clauses when meaning is clear
  - Appositives using commas, dashes, or parentheses

Proficient Anchor:
  One clear, complete sentence that fulfills the specific task — no extra information required.

🔒 APPOSITIVE IDENTIFICATION RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
An appositive MUST be:
  ✅ A NOUN PHRASE that RENAMES or IDENTIFIES another noun
  ✅ Set off by commas, dashes, or parentheses

An appositive is NOT:
  ❌ A prepositional phrase ("from Kenya, Africa")
  ❌ A participial phrase ("named Rex and Buddy")
  ❌ An adjective phrase ("tall and strong")
  ❌ A relative clause ("who lives in Texas")
  ❌ An adverbial phrase ("in the morning")

VALID appositive examples:
  "Peter Tabichi, a science teacher in Kenya, won the award."
  "Mud, a stubborn little donkey, surprised everyone at the game."
  "The Global Teacher Prize, a prestigious award, was given to Tabichi."

INVALID — these are NOT appositives:
  "Peter Tabichi, from Kenya, teaches science." (prepositional phrase)
  "Peter Tabichi, known for his kindness, teaches science." (participial phrase)
  "The teacher, who lives in a rural area, won the prize." (relative clause)

Score Ideas 0/1 if the task requires an appositive and no true noun-phrase appositive is present.

Q5 specific (Combine with because/but/so):
  target_skill_satisfied = true if:
    - the response uses because, but, or so (NOT "and," "when," "if," "while," etc.)
    - preserves the meaning of both original sentences
    - forms one grammatically complete sentence

  IMPORTANT: Only because/but/so satisfy this task. Other conjunctions (and, when, if, while, although) do NOT meet the requirement. Score Ideas 0/1 if the wrong conjunction is used.

🔒 Q6–Q10 TASK LOCK — Sentence Writing
Purpose: Write one on-topic sentence that includes support.

Requirements:
  - One complete sentence
  - On topic
  - Includes at least ONE of:
    • a clear reason (explicit "because," "so," "since," or "to…")
    • an explanation of feeling or consequence (e.g., "in fear," "feeling proud," "to help")
    • a specific example, time, or event ("when our class planted trees")
  - Implicit emotional or motivational phrases count as explanations even without a conjunction.
  🚫 Never suggest writing more than one sentence.

🔒 CIRCULAR REASONING CHECK (Q6–Q10) (NEW)
Before awarding Ideas 2/2, verify the "reason" is NOT:
  - A direct synonym or definitional restatement of the claim ("good teacher teaches well")
  - A tautology ("X is important because it matters")

If the "reason" is a LITERAL RESTATEMENT using synonyms, score Ideas 1/2.

⚠️ IMPORTANT — NOT CIRCULAR (award Ideas 2/2):
  Personal judgments, preferences, and evaluations ARE valid reasons for Q6–Q10 opinion/personal response questions. The student does NOT need to cite specific text evidence on Q6–Q10 — that is a Q11 requirement. If the student expresses a genuine opinion about WHY (even a simple one like "because it would be fun to watch"), that counts as a valid reason.

  Example — CIRCULAR (Ideas 1/2):
    Q: "What makes a good teacher?"
    A: "A good teacher is someone who teaches well."
    → "Teaches well" = synonym for "good teacher." Literal restatement.

  Example — NOT CIRCULAR (Ideas 2/2):
    Q: "What makes a good teacher?"
    A: "A good teacher is someone who makes you feel like you can do hard things."
    → Identifies a specific quality (encouragement) — genuine reason.

  Example — NOT CIRCULAR (Ideas 2/2):
    Q: "What makes a good teacher?"
    A: "A good teacher helps students believe they can succeed, even when things are hard."
    → Adds specific behavior (helps students believe) + condition (when things are hard).

🔒 Q11 TASK LOCK — Paragraph Writing (Surprise Kick)
Purpose: Explain how events cause Cody to change his opinions in one organized paragraph.

Requirements:
  - Clear main idea about HOW Cody's opinions changed (not just WHAT happened)
  - Logical order
  - ≥ 2 text-based events with explanations of how they changed Cody's thinking
  - Must address Cody's INITIAL opinions and how they SHIFTED
  - Conceptual accuracy > length or style

CRITICAL DISTINCTION — Retelling vs. Analysis:
  RETELLING (Ideas 9/15): Describes story events in order without connecting them to opinion changes. "First this happened, then that happened."
  ANALYSIS (Ideas 12–15/15): Explains HOW events made Cody THINK differently. "When Mud scored, Cody realized the donkey was tougher than he thought."

SECTION 6: Q11 CONCEPTUAL MASTERY PRIORITY
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

🧠 Conceptual Mastery Priority (Q11 only)
Prioritize conceptual understanding and logical reasoning over extra detail.
Distinguish: conceptual gap (incorrect/missing link) → deduct; elaboration gap (correct but thin) → mention only.
Evidence sufficiency: ≥ 2 specific events + brief explanations = full Ideas credit.
Reward clarity, accuracy, and organization over length or style.
Phrase improvements as extensions, not corrections.
 ✅ “You could make your paragraph even stronger by explaining how Cody felt after Mud scored.”
 When borderline, favor the higher score if meaning and organization are strong.

SECTION 7: SCORING LOGIC (internal only)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Apply invisibly; never reveal numeric values.

Q1–Q5 (Sentence Editing / Revision)
  Ideas (1 pt): 1 if the target skill is clearly met and reads closer to Proficient.
  Conventions (1 pt): 1 if capital + terminal present, no fragment/run-on, major_error_gate = false, and within error tolerance.
  ⚠️ Do NOT deduct or coach for missing reasons or explanations.
  Comparative override applies when fluency is clear despite a small slip.

Q6–Q10 (Sentence Writing)
  Ideas (2 pts):
    2 → one complete, on-topic sentence with genuine (non-circular) support
    1 → on-topic answer but no real support, or circular reasoning
    0 → off-topic or unclear
  Conventions (1 pt):
    Award independently of Ideas whenever the sentence is grammatically complete.
    Treat name slips and slightly awkward but grammatical phrasing as one minor error; do not lower Ideas for these.
    Harmless elaboration is fine if grammar and logic remain sound.

Q11 (Paragraph Writing)
Ideas (15 pts): Start 15; deduct 3 only for clear conceptual gaps (missing main idea, focus, ≥2 text-based details, explanations, or logical order). Apply the evidence sufficiency rule first. If conceptually clear but concise, keep higher credit.
 Conventions (5 pts): Start 5; deduct 1 per pattern (run-ons, punctuation, grammar/spelling, unclear sentence, missing conclusion). Ignore ≤ 2 minor surface errors if meaning is clear.
